SCIENCE 


 CHAPTER-2  
 

 
 Control and Coordination 
 

 SUMMARY 



    

  1. Movement in organisms
    2. 

      

    1. The ability of organisms to move certain body parts is movement.
      2. 

    2. When they move from one place to another, it is called locomotion.
      3. 

    3. Organisms show movements in response to stimuli.
      4. 

    


  2. Introduction to control & coordination
    3. 

      

    1. Organisms move in response to various kinds of stimuli like light, heat, nutrients/food, mate etc.
      2. 

    2. All the activities in animals are controlled and coordinated by the nervous and endocrine system.
      3. 

    3. Hormones are chemical messengers which assist the nervous system in carrying out various functions. They are secreted 
by endocrine glands.
      4. 

    4. Hormones in plants coordinate the movements.
      5. 

    


  3. THE NERVOUS SYSTEM
    4. 

      

    1. Neuron
      2. 

        

      1. Neuron is the structural and functional unit of the nervous system.
        2. 

      2. Each neuron has three main parts: dendrites, cyton/soma/cell body and axon.
        3. 

      3. Dendrites receive impulses from other neurons.
        4. 

      4. Cyton/soma processes the impulse.
        5. 

      5. Axon transmits the impulse, either to another neuron or to muscles/glands etc.
        6. 

      6. Axon may be myelinated or non-myelinated.
        7. 

      7. The impulse transmission is faster in myelinated neurons.
        8. 

      


    2. Central nervous system
      3. 

        

      1. The central nervous system (CNS) is made up of the brain and the spinal cord. 
        2. 

      2. Cerebrum is responsible for reasoning, logic, emotions, speech, memory, visual processing, recognition of auditory 
and taste stimuli, etc.
        3. 

      3. Cerebellum regulates and coordinates body movements, posture and balance.
        4. 

      4. Pons relays signals from hindbrain to forebrain.
        5. 

      5. Medulla Oblongata controls all involuntary movements like vomiting, sneezing, yawning, heartbeat, breathing, blood 
pressure, etc.
        6. 

      6. Medulla oblongata continues as the spinal cord which runs through the vertebral column and it controls reflex actions.
        7. 

      


    3. Peripheral nervous system
      4. 

        

      1. The nerves given out by the brain and the spinal cord form the peripheral nervous system (PNS).
        2. 

      2. There are 12 cranial nerves and 31 spinal nerves in humans.
        3. 

      


    4. Somatic nervous system
      5. 

        

      1. It forms a part of the PNS.
        2. 

      2. The nerves of PNS that control the voluntary actions of the body form the somatic nervous system.
        3. 

      


    5. Autonomic nervous system
      6. 

        

      1. All the nerves of the PNS that control the involuntary actions in the body form the autonomic nervous system.
        2. 

      2. Two divisions of autonomic nervous system are: sympathetic and parasympathetic nervous system
        3. 

      3. The sympathetic nervous system prepares the body for intense physical activity and is often referred to as the fight-or-
flight response while the parasympathetic nervous system has almost the exact opposite effect and relaxes the body and 
inhibits or slows many high energy functions.
        4. 

      

    


  4. Reflex action
    5. 

      

    1. Reflex action is a sudden, involuntary reaction of the body in response to stimuli.
      2. 

    


  5. Reflex arc
    6. 

      

    1. It is the path followed by electrical impulse during a reflex action.
      2. 

    2. The impulse travels from the receptor organ to the spinal cord/brain; is processed there and the information is brought back 
to the concerned muscle to carry out the action.
      3. 

    3. Thus, receptor organ, sensory/afferent neuron, interneuron, motor/efferent neuron and effector organ are the components 
of a reflex arc.
      4. 

    


  6. The Endocrine System
    7. 

      

    1. Exocrine glands 
      2. 

        

      1. Exocrine glands are glands that discharge secretions by means of  ducts, which open onto an epithelial surface.
        2. 

      


    2. Endocrine glands
      3. 

        

      1. Endocrine glands are the ductless glands which secrete hormones into the bloodstream in humans.
        2. 

      


    3. Pituitary gland
      4. 

        

      1. It is a pea-sized gland located at the base of the brain.
        2. 

      2. It is the master gland as it controls the secretions of all the other endocrine glands.
        3. 

      3. It also secretes Growth Hormone (GH). Under-secretion of GH causes ‘Dwarfism’ and over-secretion causes ‘Gigantism’ in 
children and ‘Acromegaly’ in adults.
        4. 

      


    4. Thyroid gland
      5. 

        

      1. It is a butterfly-shaped gland located in the throat.
        2. 

      2. It secretes the hormone ‘Thyroxine’ which regulates the metabolism of the body.
        3. 

      3. Iodine is required to synthesize thyroxine in the body.
        4. 

      4. In case of iodine deficiency, under-secretion of thyroxine leads to goitre.
        5. 

      


    5. Pancreas
      6. 

        

      1. It is a leaf-like gland present behind the stomach in the abdomen.
        2. 

      2. It is an endocrine as well an exocrine gland.
        3. 

      3. As an endocrine gland, it manufactures two hormones – Insulin and glucagon.
        4. 

      4.  Both these hormones act antagonistically and regulate the sugar level in the blood.
        5. 

      5. As an exocrine gland, it secretes enzymes to break down the proteins, lipids, carbohydrates, and nucleic acids in food.
        6. 

      6. Insufficient amount of insulin from pancreas leads to diabetes.
        7. 

      


    6. Adrenal gland
      7. 

        

      1. Occurs in pair above each kidney.
        2. 

      2. It decreases in size with age.
        3. 

      3. Secrets the hormone adrenaline which helps in flight and fight response.
        4. 

      4. Also secretes nor adrenaline
        5. 

      


    7. Gonads
      8. 

        

      1. Gonads are the gamete-producing organs – testes in males and ovaries in females.
        2. 

      2. The testes produce the male hormone testosterone and ovaries produce the female hormones oestrogen and progesterone.
        3. 

      3. Testosterone and oestrogen help in producing gametes and are responsible for the sexual characteristics in males and 
females respectively.
        4. 

      4. Progesterone is the pregnancy hormone.
        5. 

      

    


  7. Other endocrine organs
    8. 

      

    1. The other endocrine organs include the hypothalamus, parathyroid, pineal and thymus glands.
      2.
