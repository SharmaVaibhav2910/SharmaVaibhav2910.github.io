 Heredity and Evolution 
 

 SUMMARY 



    

  1. Sexual reproduction 
    2. 

      

    1. The mode of reproduction that involves two individuals; one male and one female.
      2. 

    2. They produce sex cells or gametes which fuse to form a new organism.
      3. 

    


  2. Genes 
    3. 

      

    1. Gene is the functional unit of heredity.
      2. 

    2. Every gene controls one or several particular characteristic features in living organisms.
      3. 

    


  3. Heredity 
    4. 

      

    1. The process by which the features of an organism is passed on from one generation to another is called heredity.
      2. 

    2. The tall trait of the plant is said to be dominant over the short trait.
      3. 

    


  4. Recessive traits 
    5. 

      

    1. A trait which is not expressed in presence of a dominant allele is known as recessive.
      2. 

    2. So, recessive character/trait is present in an organism but cannot be seen if a dominant allele exists.
      3. 

    


  5. Monohybrid cross 
    6. 

      

    1. When only one character is considered while crossing two organisms, then such a cross is known as monohybrid cross.
      2. 

    2. The ratio of characters, arising out of this cross, at F2 generation is called monohybrid ratio.
      3. 

    3. E.g., If tall plant (TT) is crossed with a dwarf plant (tt), we get 3 tall:1 short plant at the end of the F2 generation.
      4. 

    4. So, 3:1 is monohybrid ratio.
      5. 

    5. Here, the height of the plant is considered at a time.
      6. 

    


  6. Dihybrid cross 
    7. 

      

    1. When two characters are considered while crossing two organisms, then such a cross is known as a dihybrid cross.
      2. 

    2. The ratio of characters, arising out of this cross, at F2 generation is called dihybrid ratio.
      3. 

    3. E.g., If a plant with round and green pea is crossed with a plant with wrinkled and yellow pea,
      4. 

    4. The first generation plants would all have round and green pea.
      5. 

    5. On crossing the same for an F2 generation, we would observe four combinations of characters in the ratio of 9:3:3:1.
      6. 

    6. Thus, 9:3:3:1 is the dihybrid ratio.
      7. 

    


  7. Inheritance 
    8. 

      

    1. In Biology, inheritance pertains to the transfer of traits from one generation to another.
      2. 

    


  8. Sex determination 
    9. 

      

    1. The process of determining the sex of an individual based on the composition of the genetic material is called sex 
determination.
      2. 

    2. In different animals, sex of an embryo is determined by different factors.
      3. 

    3. In humans, sex determination happens on the basis of the presence or absence of Y chromosome.
      4. 

    4. XX is female and XY is male
      5. 

    5. An ovum always contains X chromosome.
      6. 

    6. An Ovum, upon fusion with Y containing sperm, gives rise to a male child and upon fusion with X containing sperm 
gives rise to a girl child.
      7. 

    


  9. Traits 
    10. 

      

    1. Traits are characteristic features of an organism, manifested in a physical form that is visible or in a physiological 
aspect of the organism.
      2. 

    


  10. Acquired characters 
    11. 

      

    1. The traits that are acquired by an organism over the period of its lifetime are termed as acquired characters.
      2. 

    2. These characters may or may not get transferred to the next generation.
      3. 

    


  11. Inherited characters
    12. 

      

    1. The traits that are inherited from the parents are called inherited characters.
      2. 

    2. These traits always get transferred to the next generation, but depending on the dominance or recessiveness it may or 
may not be expressed.
      3. 

    3. Examples are height, skin colour, and eye colour.
      4. 

    


  12. Variation 
    13.  

      

    1. Genetic variations 
      2. 

        

      1. The differences in the DNA sequences among every organism leading to the diverse gene pool are called genetic variations. 
        2. 

      2. These differences lead to different/varied physical characters or biochemical pathways.
        3. 

      


    2. Natural selection
      3. 

        

      1. It is the phenomenon by which a favourable trait in a population of a species is selected.
        2. 

      2. Changing natural conditions exert equal pressure on all the existing species.
        3. 

      3. The species/organisms which are better adapted to the changing conditions survive and reproduce i.e. selected by nature 
and species/organisms which cannot adapt perish i.e. rejected by nature.
        4. 

      

    


  13. Speciation 
    14.  

      

    1. Genetic drift
      2. 

        

      1. Natural selection can play an important role in deciding the traits that survive in a population.
        2. 

      2.  However, random fluctuations in gene variants are seen on many occasions. This phenomenon is known as genetic drift. 
        3. 

      3. Thus, genetic drift is a change in the frequency of an existing allele in a small population.
        4. 

      4. Genetic drift may cause a gene variant to disappear from the population and thus reduce genetic variation.
        5. 

      


    2. Speciation
      3. 

        

      1. It is the process of formation of a new species from existing ones due to several evolutionary forces like genetic drift, 
isolation of populations, natural selection etc. 
        2.  

      2. Speciation leads to diversity in the ecosystem and the diversity and diversity lead to evolution.
        3. 

      


    3. Gene flow
      4. 

        

      1. Gene flow is the transfer of genes from one population to the next.
        2. 

      


    4. Population
      5. 

        

      1. A population is a community or a group of animals, plants or any living organism that can reproduce with each other 
and have fertile, viable offsprings.
        2. 

      

    


  14. What Happen to Body after the Death 
    15. 

      

    1. Organisms die and they get buried in mud and silt.
      2. 

    2. The soft tissues of the body get quickly leaving behind the hard bones or shells
      3. 

    3. Over time sediments build over it and harden into rock
      4. 

    4. As the bones decay, mineral seep in to replace the contents cell by cell, process called as petrification
      5. 

    5. If bones decay completely, it leaves behind the cast of the animal.
      6.
